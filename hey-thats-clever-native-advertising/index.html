Playing on a Grid

I'm going to assume that most of the people who've found my little spot on the net know just what native advertising is. But I'm also going to assume that some of the people don't.

Here's a simple definition: native advertising is when a content creator presents paid content in the style of said content creator.

It's more effective, because it seems natural — native even. According to Mashable, the phrase and its current for has been hanging around advertisers since late 2011 (Todd Wasserman, 25 September 2012, http://mashable.com/2012/09/25/native-advertising/). I know I've been hearing about it since 2013 or so, when I was working at the Boston Globe and they were presenting ads as articles and editorials. It's a constant dynamic tension — the advertisers, who are paying for camouflage and the content people, who want to separate themselves as much as possible.

I did The Princeton Review's treatment for some of their pages in this vein: The Center for Study Abroad, and The Gap Year Experience: A Life-Changing Opportunity are examples. In both cases, the page formatting was the same, and a few call-out were made. In a heavy text site, some more text blends in, but it's up at the top, so it's obvious. Personally, I like the presentation of the second was superior to the first, but we weren't able to agree on a way for the heading to look. That's tension between developers, designers, and marketers.

Now see this? I came across that Tuesday (16 February 2016) on Wired.com. Unfortunately, I don't remember which article. Honestly, it doesn't matter that much. This is clever. It's an advert, on their site, disguised as one of their adverts for one of their articles on their site.

There's even an in-character link! If you, welcome reader, look closely at the image, you will see that they mention a partner site: wired.com/partners/lexluthor.

Hey That's Clever: Native Advertising.
